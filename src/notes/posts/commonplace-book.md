For most of the last three centuries, careful readers kept what was called a **commonplace book**: a ruled volume into which they copied, by hand, any passage worth keeping.<label for="mn-1" class="margin-toggle">&#8853;</label><input type="checkbox" id="mn-1" class="margin-toggle"><span class="marginnote">Locke, Erasmus, and Marcus Aurelius all kept some version of one. The labor was the point.

> The page remembers so the mind doesn't have to. That is the whole bargain, and it is a better one than it looks.

## Why the form matters

Copying a sentence out is slow, and the slowness does something a highlight never will.<label for="sn-2" class="margin-toggle sidenote-number"></label><input type="checkbox" id="sn-2" class="margin-toggle"><span class="sidenote">Highlighting feels like work and produces almost none of its benefit Ã¢â‚¬â€ the great cognitive sleight of hand of the e-reader era.</span> You feel the shape of the prose under your hand. You notice where the argument turns. By the time you've finished transcribing, you have half-memorized it and fully understood it.

A few habits make the practice hold together:

- Copy the passage, then write one line in your own words beneath it.
- Date everything. Later, the dates become a second story.
- Don't organize too early. Let connections surface on their own.

What you end up with is not a filing system but a record of attention Ã¢â‚¬â€ proof of where your mind actually went over a year. Reread it in December and you'll find a version of yourself you'd half-forgotten, still arguing with a book you no longer remember finishing.

The tools barely matter. A paper notebook works. A plain text file works. The discipline travels better than the artifact, which is the encouraging part: you can start the next time you read anything worth keeping. You can read more in [the original sources](#), but you already have everything you need.
